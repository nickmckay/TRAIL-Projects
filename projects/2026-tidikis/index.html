Integrating AI into Cognitive Psychology and Psychology of Creativity

Redesigning two psychology courses to embrace AI as a tool for learning rather than a threat to it.

Presented by Viktoria Tidikis, Department of Psychological Sciences, NAU

About This Project

When generative AI emerged, Viktoria Tidikis's first reaction was wonder, and her second was uncertainty about what it meant for the courses she teaches. Recognizing that students could now produce polished papers in seconds, she stepped back and asked a deeper question: what does it actually mean to learn, and how has that always changed with technology? Drawing on a historical arc from cave paintings through the printing press to the internet, she reframed AI not as a crisis for education but as the latest in a long series of shifts that required educators to adapt. The result was a redesigned approach to Cognitive Psychology and Psychology of Creativity that integrates AI as a genuine tool for thinking rather than something to police.

Tidikis restructured assignments so that students engage with AI in ways that require critical evaluation and creative judgment, not mere generation. In the creativity course, for example, students used AI image tools to reconstruct personal memories, then reflected on which AI-generated images captured the emotional truth of their recollections and why. This kind of task cannot be delegated to the AI itself: it demands that students articulate something personal and evaluate output against an internal standard. Her project explores how faculty can redesign assessments and course goals to develop genuinely AI-era competencies, including the judgment to know when and how to use these tools effectively.
