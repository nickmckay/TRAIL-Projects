From Perils to Pedagogy: Teaching AI Literacy in COM 101

Turning the "college essay is dead" panic into a genuine learning opportunity in introductory communication.

Presented by Jermaine Martinez, Department of Communication, NAU

About This Project

Jermaine Martinez opens his project by noting that almost every new communication technology in history has been called a revolution in education, from Thomas Edison's film in 1922 to radio in the 1930s to educational television. None of them reshaped schooling in the ways their champions predicted. Generative AI is different in one respect: rather than being heralded as a revolution, it has more often been met with dystopic alarm, particularly around academic integrity. Martinez's project in COM 101, a large lecture course of roughly 300 students, took a different approach. Drawing on semiotic theory, media ecology, and the concept of "super abundance" (the vast human archive of symbolic communication that large language models are trained on), he redesigned units to help students critically analyze what AI actually is, where it comes from, and what it reveals about human communication itself.

The redesigned course used AI as an object of study, not just a tool. Students examined how ChatGPT's outputs reflect patterns in human language and culture, investigated algorithmic bias and hallucinations through a communication lens, and completed assignments that required them to evaluate AI-generated content against their own communicative knowledge. Martinez's approach demonstrates that introductory communication courses are well-positioned to teach AI literacy, because the field already has the frameworks: semiosis, media as environment, the gap between the map and the territory.
